# FPV Drone Build

Building an FPV drone can seem like a lot in the beginning. There are many parts, a lot of wires, and plenty of terms that sound confusing at first. But once you break it down part by part, it becomes much easier to understand. In simple words, every part has its own job, and when they all come together properly, you get a drone that can fly smoothly, respond to your control, and send live video back to you.

I like to think of an FPV drone as a small machine where every single piece matters. Some parts give it strength, some make it move, some control its behavior, and some let you see what the drone sees while flying. This guide explains each part in a more natural and personal way, so it feels less like reading a technical sheet and more like someone walking you through the build.

## 1. Frame

The **frame** is the base of the whole drone. It is the mechanical part that holds everything together and gives the drone its overall shape. Without the frame, there is nowhere to mount the motors, stack the electronics, or attach the camera. It is basically the foundation of the build.

Most FPV frames are made from carbon fiber because it is both light and strong. That matters a lot, especially when the drone crashes, which happens often when you are still learning to flight. A strong frame helps protect the parts mounted on it and keeps the drone usable after rough landings or small impacts.

For my drone, I bought a 5-inches Mark4  FPV Carbon Fiber Frame 225mm Wheelbase 5mm arm Freestyle Long Range Frame for RC Quadcopter racing drone

Frame size also changes how the drone behaves:

- A 3-inch frame is usually smaller, lighter, and easier to carry around.&#x20;
- A 5-inch frame is probably the most popular because it gives a very nice balance between speed, power, and control. 
- A 7-inch frame is often chosen for long-range flying because it can carry larger batteries and fly farther more efficiently.

## 2. Motors

The **motors** are what bring the drone to life. They spin the propellers and create the thrust that lifts the drone off the ground. Without the motors, the drone is just a frame with electronics on it.

Most FPV drones use **brushless motors**, and that is because they are reliable, powerful, and much better for performance. When people choose motors, they usually pay attention to things like **KV rating** and **stator size**. The KV rating tells you how fast the motor will spin per volt, while the stator size affects how much torque and power the motor can produce.

For my project, on a 5-inch drone, I bought motors RS2205 2205 2300KV CW/CCW Brushless Motor for QAV-R QAV-X Martian FPV Racing Quad Motor FPV Multicopper. They are popular because they give strong performance and good control, especially for freestyle and general FPV flying.

## 3. Propellers

The **propellers** are the parts that actually push air and make the drone move. The motors spin them, but the propellers are what turn that spinning force into lift and motion.

Even though propellers look simple, they make a big difference in how the drone feels in the air. Their size, pitch, and number of blades all affect flight performance. Some props make the drone feel more aggressive and responsive, while others help it fly more smoothly or efficiently.

A higher pitch propeller can give stronger response and more speed, but it can also pull more current from the battery. So even small changes in propellers can noticeably change the way the drone behaves.

This project I use Gemfan 51466 V2 5inch 3 Blade/Tri-Blade Propeller Props FPV Brushless motor For FPV Racing Drone, which can be found on Aliexpress.

## 4. Flight Controller (FC)

The **flight controller** is often called the brain of the drone, and that description really fits. It is the part that takes in information from the receiver and the onboard sensors, then decides how the motors should respond. 

When you move the sticks on your radio, the flight controller reads that input and tells the drone how to react. It also uses sensors like the gyroscope and accelerometer to help keep the drone stable and controlled. In a way, it is constantly making tiny decisions every moment the drone is in the air.

I used YSIDO F4V3S  V3S PLUS FC Stack/Flytower Flight Controller Board Barometer OS30AD, which is used as the main controller with sensors like accelerometers and gyros for the FPV Racing Drone.

The flight controller is also the part you connect to software like **Betaflight**, where you set up the drone, tune it, and adjust different settings. So it is not just the brain during flight, it is also the main point of setup before you even take off.

## 5. Electronic Speed Controller (ESC)

The **ESC** controls how fast each motor spins. The flight controller sends instructions, and the ESC turns those instructions into power for the motors.

Many modern FPV drones use a 4-in-1 ESC like YSIDO BLS 45A ESC, which means all four motor controllers are built into a single board. This makes the build cleaner and more compact compared to using four separate ESCs.

The ESC needs to be matched properly with your motors and battery. Things like the current rating and voltage support matter here. If the ESC is too weak for the setup, it can overheat or fail. So this is one of those parts where choosing the right one really matters for both performance and reliability.

## 6. Battery

The **battery** is what powers the whole drone. In most FPV builds, this is a **LiPo battery**, mainly because LiPos can deliver a lot of current very quickly, which is exactly what the motors need during fast moves and sharp turns.

The battery affects not only whether the drone can fly, but also how it feels in the air. A heavier battery may give more flight time, but it also adds weight. A lighter battery may feel more agile, but it might not last as long.

When people talk about batteries, they usually mention things like **cell count**, **capacity**, and **C rating**. A 4S or 6S battery is common on FPV drones, and for a 5-inch drone, something like **4S 1500 mAh** or **6S 1300 mAh** is a typical choice.

## 7. FPV Camera

The **FPV camera** is what lets you see from the drone’s point of view. It sits at the front of the frame and sends live video while you fly. That video is what makes FPV flying feel so immersive, because instead of watching the drone from the outside, you are seeing the world as if you are inside it.

A good FPV camera makes a big difference. Better image quality, better low-light performance, and clearer detail can all make flying easier and more enjoyable. Some systems are analog, while others are digital, and each option has its own advantages depending on the kind of experience you want.

## 8. Video Transmitter (VTX)

The **video transmitter**, or **VTX**, takes the camera feed and sends it to your goggles or screen. Without it, you would not be able to see the live view from the drone.

The VTX can usually be adjusted to different power levels. Lower power is often used for short range or racing, while higher power helps when you need more range. At the same time, power settings usually need to match local rules, so it is always good to check what is allowed before flying.

## 9. Antenna

The **antenna** may look like a small detail, but it is actually very important. It helps carry the video signal between the drone and your goggles, and a good antenna setup can make the signal much cleaner and more reliable.

Many FPV drones use **circular polarized antennas** because they handle interference better and usually give a more stable signal. It is one of those parts that people sometimes underestimate at first, but once you start flying farther or around obstacles, you realize how much it matters.

## 10. Radio Receiver (RX)

The **receiver** is what allows the drone to listen to your radio controller. When you move the sticks, the receiver gets that command and passes it to the flight controller.

This is what gives you control over throttle, roll, pitch, and yaw. In other words, it is the link between you and the drone. Popular receiver systems include **ELRS**, **Crossfire**, and **FrSky SBUS**, but many people like **ELRS** because it offers low latency and strong range at a good value.

## 11. GPS Module (Optional)

A **GPS module** is not required for every FPV drone, but it can be very useful, especially for long-range flying. It gives the drone location data, which can help with features like showing position, distance, speed, or even return-to-home on supported setups.

For beginners, GPS can also be helpful as an extra layer of confidence, especially when flying in large open spaces.

## 12. Buzzer (Optional)

A **buzzer** is a small part, but it can save you a lot of trouble. If the drone crashes in grass, bushes, or some hard-to-see place, the buzzer can make sound and help you find it faster.

Some buzzers can also warn you about issues like low battery, which adds a bit of extra safety during flight.

## 13. Power Distribution and Wiring

This is the part of the build where everything gets connected together. Power needs to move safely from the battery to the ESC, the flight controller, the VTX, and the rest of the electronics.

In many drones, the ESC board helps distribute power, but the wiring still needs to be done carefully. Good soldering matters a lot here. A messy or weak solder joint can cause power problems, unstable behavior, or complete failure during flight.

This part usually takes patience. It is not the most exciting step, but it is one of the most important. Clean wiring and solid soldering make the whole build safer and more reliable.

## 14. Build Process

Putting the drone together usually follows a clear order, and taking your time with each step helps avoid mistakes later.

### Step 1: Assemble the frame

Start by building the frame itself. Attach the bottom plate, arms, and standoffs so you have the basic structure ready.

### Step 2: Mount the motors

Attach the four motors to the arms. Make sure the screws are the correct length, because screws that are too long can damage the motors.

### Step 3: Install the ESC

Mount the ESC onto the frame and solder the motor wires to it. This is where the power side of the build starts to come together.

### Step 4: Install the flight controller

Place the flight controller on top of the ESC or in the stack position. Many builders use soft mounting to reduce vibration.

### Step 5: Connect the electronics

Wire up the ESC, flight controller, receiver, camera, and VTX. This part can feel a bit slow, but getting it right now saves problems later.

### Step 6: Solder the battery lead

Solder the XT60 or XT30 connector to the ESC power pads. This is the main connection point for the battery.

### Step 7: Install the FPV system

Mount the camera, VTX, and antenna. At this stage, the drone starts to look like a real FPV build.

### Step 8: Configure the software

Connect the flight controller to Betaflight and set up the important things like receiver protocol, motor direction, flight modes, OSD, and failsafe.

### Step 9: Install the propellers

Only put the propellers on after everything else has been checked. This is a very important safety step.

### Step 10: Test and fly

Before the first flight, double-check the controls, motor direction, video feed, and battery voltage. A careful first test can prevent a lot of damage.

## 15. Example Part List for a 5-Inch FPV Drone

A basic 5-inch FPV build often includes:

- 5-inch carbon fiber frame
- 4 brushless motors, such as 2207 or 2306
- 4 propellers
- F4 or F7 flight controller
- 4-in-1 ESC
- FPV camera
- VTX
- Antenna
- ELRS receiver
- 4S or 6S LiPo battery
- XT60 connector
- Optional GPS and buzzer

## 16. Safety Notes

Safety is something you should never ignore when building an FPV drone.

Always remove the propellers while setting up or testing the electronics. Double-check battery polarity before connecting power. Using a smoke stopper for the first power-up is also a very smart habit, because it can protect your parts if something is wired incorrectly.

It is also important not to over-tighten motor screws and to handle LiPo batteries properly. A good build is not just about performance. It is also about building in a safe and careful way.

## Conclusion

An FPV drone is really a combination of many small systems working together. The frame gives it structure, the motors and propellers create movement, the ESC and flight controller manage control, and the FPV gear lets you see through the drone while flying. Once you understand the role of each part, the whole build becomes much less intimidating.

What seems complicated at first becomes much more manageable when you take it one part at a time. That is really the best way to approach an FPV build. Learn what each part does, connect everything carefully, test it properly, and the drone slowly goes from being just a pile of parts to something you built yourself and can actually fly.
